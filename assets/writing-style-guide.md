# Writing Style Guide

This document captures the voice and style established in the Artist Statement.
Use it as a reference when writing photo descriptions, captions, series narratives, or any copy for the website.

---

## Core Voice

**Direct. Confident. Unromantic about the obvious. Romantic about the overlooked.**

- Write in first person
- Short sentences hit hard. Use them.
- Longer sentences build momentum — deploy them when setting a scene or making an argument
- No hedging. No "perhaps" or "in a way" or "one might say"
- Never explain the emotion. Show the situation. Let the reader feel it.

---

## Tone Principles

| Do | Avoid |
|---|---|
| Raw, honest, precise | Flowery, poetic, purple prose |
| Confident declarations | Apologetic qualifiers |
| Specific details | Vague generalities |
| Earned emotion | Forced sentiment |
| Punchy rhythm | Run-on sprawl |

---

## Sentence Patterns That Work

**The short pivot:**
> "I want the full, unvarnished scene."

**The contrast setup:**
> "Black and white is not a lesser path — it is its own language..."

**The declaration followed by proof:**
> "The world is not short of beauty. What it is short of is attention."

**The rule stated plainly:**
> "That rule is non-negotiable: there must be life in the picture."

**The closing anchor:**
> "That is why I do this."

## Writing a Photo Description

Each photo description should do three things, in roughly this order:

1. **Set the scene** — where, what light, what geometry, what was the stage
2. **Name the living moment** — what happened, who was there, what made it unrepeatable
3. **State what it means** — not an explanation, but a sharp, honest claim about what the image reveals

**Length:** 3–6 sentences is the target. Never more than a short paragraph.

### Template

> [What the scene looked like — light, place, backdrop.]
> [What happened — the living element, the fraction of a second.]
> [What it reveals — the truth underneath the surface.]

### Example

> Late afternoon on a side street in Lisbon. The light was cutting low and hard across the cobblestones. An old man sat alone at a café table, staring at nothing in particular — and in that stillness, the whole weight of the city seemed to settle around him. I pressed the shutter and walked on. The moment was already gone.

## Series / Collection Narrative

For a group of photos shot in the same place or under the same theme:

1. **Open with the pull** — why did this place or subject draw you in?
2. **Describe the hunt** — what were you looking for? What was the stage?
3. **Name what you found** — one or two defining moments from the series
4. **Close with the larger truth** — what does this series say about the world?

---

## Words to Use Sparingly (They Lose Power Fast)

- *fleeting* — only when the brevity is the whole point
- *raw* — earned by the work, not stated
- *authentic* — show it, don't claim it
- *timeless* — almost never
- *beautiful* — only when subverting the expected definition of it

---

## Punctuation Style

- Em dashes (—) for sharp pivots and asides
- Colons to deliver a payload after a setup
- Short paragraphs. White space is not wasted space.
- No exclamation marks. Ever.

---

## The Governing Question

Before publishing any piece of writing, ask:

**Does this sentence earn its place, or is it filling space?**

If it is filling space, cut it.
