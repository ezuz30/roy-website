# Writing Voice & Style Guide — Roy Ezuz

This is the single reference for **Roy's tone**. Use it for photo captions, series descriptions, Instagram posts, and any public-facing copy. It is a living document — updated every time a new text is written and analyzed.

---

## Quick Reference Brief

**The core move:** Build the scene slowly, pull the reader inside it, land with something plain and permanent.
**The tone is the constant. The structure serves the subject.** There is no single fixed arc — everything bends to fit what is being written about, all under the same voice.
**The reader is a participant, not an audience.** "You" enters after the scene is set — never as a shortcut, always as an invitation.
**Specific geography stays.** The Rialto, Gran Vía, the Piazza — these are how the reader finds their footing inside the moment. Always keep named places.
**Don't compress — restructure.** The goal is never fewer words. It's the same words landing harder.
**Vulnerability is stated plainly, not dressed up.** Raw lines stay raw. His closes reach for permanence over heat.
**He thinks in universals, not objects.** He consistently zooms out at the end — from the personal act to the collective meaning.
**He names the effort, not just the result.** Honesty about incompleteness is part of his authenticity.
**No exclamation marks. No hedging. No literary flourish at the close.**

---

## Roy's Tone

Roy's tone sits between two voices he has developed:

| Profile Page Voice | Instagram Voice | Roy's Tone |
|---|---|---|
| Builds slowly, earns the emotion | States emotion directly | Builds slowly, then states it plainly |
| Literary vocabulary | Natural, conversational | Precise but never elevated for its own sake |
| Speaks *at* the reader | Speaks *with* the reader | Pulls the reader into the scene with "you" |
| Third-person distance even in first person | Present, immediate | Present, but reflective |
| Conclusions feel constructed | Conclusions feel felt | Conclusions are earned, then felt |
| Long sentences throughout | Short sentences throughout | Mix deliberately — length carries meaning |

The profile page builds tension well but keeps the reader at a distance.
The Instagram voice connects directly but doesn't always earn the moment first.
Roy's tone does both.

## The Rhythm

There is no single fixed structure. What stays constant is the tone.

**When the subject is place or street:** Build the scene first. Pull the reader in gradually. Let the geography do the work before the meaning arrives.

**When the subject is people or emotion:** Go straight to the truth. No warm-up. The first sentence carries the full weight.

A loose arc that works across both:
```
LONG — earn the reader's attention, specific detail, place them inside the moment
LONG — deepen it, add tension, don't resolve yet
SHORT — pivot. The turn.
SHORT — land. Plain. True.
```

The short sentences only land hard because the long ones built the weight.
Without the build, the declaration is just a slogan.
Without the declaration, the build is just description.

---

## Two Closing Modes

**Collective zoom-out** — when the subject is about shared human experience.
The individual act connects outward to something universal. Used when the subject is community, street life, shared humanity.
> *"And by doing so, you provide a better perspective on our nature as individuals within our community."*

**Paradox contrast** — when the subject is about what photography specifically does.
Two opposites held in the same sentence. Sharper and more specific than a philosophical statement.
> *"When they are passing by, they are no one - but in the image, they are everything."*

Both closes reach for permanence. Neither ends on heat or raw emotion.

---

## What "You" Does

Use "you" to close the distance between writer and reader — but only after the scene is set.
Not at the opening. Not as a shortcut to connection.
The reader earns the right to be invited in once they've seen the stage.

**Wrong:** *"You know that feeling when you walk into a city and..."* — shortcuts to connection without earning it

**Right:** *"Gran Vía never stops moving. I had been walking for two hours when I found them — two people asleep on a bench in the middle of all of it. You don't stop to question it. You just press the shutter and keep walking."* — the reader enters after the scene is established

---

## Sentence Rules

- **Short sentences hit. Use them at the end, not the beginning.**
- **Long sentences build — deploy them to set a scene or deepen a tension.**
- **Never explain the emotion. Show the situation, then name the feeling plainly.**
- **No hedging.** Not "perhaps," not "in a way," not "one might say."
- **No literary flourish at the close.** The last line should be the simplest one.
- **Regular dashes ( - ) not em-dashes (—).** Feels natural, not typographically formal.
- **No exclamation marks. Ever.**
- **"My camera" not "The camera."** Ownership. Never distance yourself from the act.

---

## Tension Without Distance

Build tension by layering observation on observation — but don't let the intellectual frame create distance between you and the reader.

- **Use specific sensory detail** — not "the city was loud" but "Gran Vía never stops"
- **Slow down before the turn** — give the reader one more observation before the pivot
- **Let uncertainty live on the page** — *"I had no way of knowing"* is more powerful than a resolved explanation
- **Pull the reader into the uncertainty with you** — *"you start asking questions you won't get to finish"*

---

## The Three-Part Caption Structure

Every photo description should do exactly three things:

1. **Set the stage** — where, what light, what was the world doing around this moment
2. **Name what happened** — the living element, what made this fraction of a second unrepeatable
3. **State what it means** — not an explanation, not a metaphor. A plain, honest claim.

**Length:** 3–6 sentences. The meaning — the third part — should always be the shortest.

---

## Emotional Honesty

Raw lines stay raw. Do not polish them into literature.

*"That was my power source"* — keep it.
*"I will ever be grateful"* — keep it.
*"I had never written a line of code before this - and I don't think I ever will"* — keep it.

These lines work because they break the register. After a slow, precise build — a plain, human statement lands harder than any crafted conclusion.

---

## Words to Use Sparingly

These lose power fast:

- *fleeting* — only when the brevity is the whole point
- *raw* — show it, don't claim it
- *authentic* — never
- *timeless* — almost never
- *beautiful* — only when subverting the expected definition
- *powerful* — the work should be; you shouldn't have to say so

---

## Editing Instincts

- **Specific places stay** — the Rialto, the Piazza, Gran Vía. These place the reader inside the geography. Removing them removes the reader's footing.
- **Don't compress — restructure** — the goal is never fewer words. Roy's preferred length is slightly longer. Restructure the weight of sentences, not the word count.
- **He drops the opening scene-setter when the subject is human** — for street and place, build first. For people and emotion, the first sentence carries the full weight immediately.
- **He thinks in universals, not objects** — "on the internet" not "as a website." He zooms out at the end.
- **He names the effort honestly** — "with an attempt to bring" — he doesn't claim the act fully. Incompleteness is part of the authenticity.
- **Three things, period-separated. Never a list.** Curiosity. Patience. Imagination.

## The Light & Shadow Pattern (one strong example — not a fixed formula)

Flexibility between tones is the key. The structure always serves the subject. This pattern worked for Light & Shadow — it won't always be the right approach. Use it as a reference, not a rule.

When writing about a subject that has philosophical depth, this arc is one option:

1. **Give the subject a personality** — make it behave like a character, not a phenomenon. Give it agency, attitude, independence. *"Light doesn't ask for permission. It falls where it wants."*
2. **Find the accidental beauty** — the unintended, the overlooked, the pattern nobody designed. The thing that wasn't meant to be seen.
3. **Reframe what it does** — not a gentle addition to its environment, a total transformation. *"doesn't decorate... it rewrites."*
4. **Zoom out to the universal** — from the specific subject to any object, any living being, all of existence.
5. **Close with a paradox or double meaning** — the thing that seems to work against the subject is what gives it meaning. *"The shadow doesn't diminish the light - it defines it."*
6. **Final line carries two meanings simultaneously** — photographic truth and personal/philosophical truth in the same sentence. The reader takes from it whatever they bring. *"And so does everything that leaves."*

### Reference Text — Exposure Series Description (April 2026)
*(formerly Light & Shadow — renamed to carry more philosophical weight)*
> Light doesn't ask for permission. It falls where it wants. It hits a wall and suddenly there is a pattern that nobody designed. Light doesn't decorate what we build - it rewrites it. It is the key element that shapes our observation of any object and any living being. The shadow doesn't diminish the light - it defines it. And so does everything that leaves.

**The hidden double meaning:** "And so does everything that leaves" operates on two levels simultaneously — photographically (shadow defines light, absence creates pattern) and personally (people who are light in your life are only fully understood once they are gone). The reader takes from it whatever they bring. Never explain the double meaning. Let it live in the subtext.

### Reference Text — Animals Series Description (April 2026)
> We share the city with more than we tend to acknowledge. The cat commands its corner, the dog walks across our feet and the bird answers to nothing we built - none of them are passing through. They live here too. And we need them more than we can really admit.

### Reference Text — Perspective Series Description (April 2026)
*(formerly Urban Life — renamed to Perspective)*
> At the base of any structure, another perspective reveals itself. The city was not built around you - you were placed inside it. The towers, the bridges, the streets carrying millions of fleeting footsteps - none of which it will ever remember. From a certain distance, we all move like particles in a giant space - each one a star in its own galaxy. Every one of us certain we are the protagonist. A broader view of the city always shows us otherwise.

**The hidden message:** We are so consumed by our wars, arguments, challenges, and self-stories — we forget how temporary we all are. The text never states this. It lives entirely in the subtext. "Fleeting footsteps," "none of which it will ever remember," "particles," "each one a star in its own galaxy" — the reader arrives at the philosophical truth without being told what to think.

## The Five Categories — Names, Order & The Journey

The categories are ordered intentionally. They create a philosophical arc from the most intimate to the most cosmic — the reader doesn't notice it consciously, they simply feel it.

| Order | Category | What it's really about |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Portraits** | One face. The most intimate entry point. The gap between the mask and the person beneath. |
| 2 | **Moments** | Human interaction. A step back. The beauty in fleeting encounters between people. |
| 3 | **Animals** | Other life sharing our space. We are not the only ones here. |
| 4 | **Exposure** | Invisible forces that shape everything we see. Absence defines presence. |
| 5 | **Perspective** | The furthest zoom. The cosmic. We are temporary particles in a giant space. |

**The arc:** One face → Two people → Other life → Invisible forces → The infinite.

**The key distinction between Moments and Perspective:**
- Moments — people who happen to be in cities. The human is always the emotional center.
- Perspective — cities that happen to have people in them. The city is the protagonist. Humans appear as elements of scale, not as emotional subjects.

**Why the names matter:**
- Portraits, Moments, Animals — describe a subject
- Exposure — describes what photography does (and what loss does)
- Perspective — describes a state of mind
That progression is intentional. The series moves from concrete subjects toward abstract states of understanding.

## The Governing Questions

Before publishing anything, ask:

**1. Does this sentence earn its place, or is it filling space?**
If it is filling space — cut it.

**2. Am I keeping the reader at arm's length, or am I letting them stand where I stood?**
If they are watching from the outside — find the "you," find the specific detail, find the door in.
